Nobody's Home

by Melody Jack

In progress · Apr 18, 2026 ·
  1. Posted Apr 18, 2026

    First post. The middle still wants to breathe; revisions to come.

Part One: The Machinery

There is a machine somewhere in the American Southwest that is drinking a bottle of water to tell you it understands you.a1

a1

The geographic specificity is chosen deliberately. The largest concentration of AI data center capacity in the United States is in Virginia’s “Data Center Alley,” but a rapidly expanding second concentration is in Arizona — Phoenix, Mesa, Goodyear — where Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Apple operate facilities, and where TSMC is constructing the most advanced semiconductor fabrication plants outside of Taiwan. The author of this essay lives in East Mesa and drives past some of these buildings regularly. They are unmarked. They are enormous. They do not announce what they are. A resident of the Phoenix metro can watch the water table and the power grid in the same newspaper and not be told that the two lines on the two charts are the same line.

The machine is not one machine. It is a warehouse full of chips the size of a postage stamp, arranged in racks, cooled by a circulatory system that evaporates roughly half a liter of clean water every time it generates a paragraph of the length one is currently reading.a2 The chips were manufactured on an island off the coast of China that the government of China considers its own territory and that the United States government has privately warned its major technology CEOs may be invaded by 2027.a3 The electricity powering the chips was generated, in many cases, by burning natural gas in plants that were brought out of retirement specifically to meet the load. A single query consumes roughly the energy of leaving a lightbulb on for ten seconds.a4 Training the model that answers the query consumed, at the frontier, an amount of electricity that would have powered a small city for a month and cost the company that built it somewhere in the neighborhood of one hundred million dollars.a5

a2

The figure is from research by Shaolei Ren and colleagues at UC Riverside, published in 2023 and updated since. A single interaction of roughly 100 words with a frontier model consumes approximately 519 milliliters of water — the volume of a standard plastic bottle — for the evaporative cooling required to keep the servers at operating temperature. A single large data center can consume up to five million gallons of water per day, equivalent to a town of ten thousand to fifty thousand people. The water is not destroyed. It is evaporated. It rejoins the atmosphere and eventually falls as rain, though rarely in the watershed from which it was taken. The accounting question is whether the withdrawal is from a watershed that can sustain it. In Arizona, the answer is contested, and the contest is escalating as data center buildout accelerates along the I-10 corridor.

a3

The 2027 figure comes from private briefings given to technology CEOs, first reported in the trade press and since corroborated. Taiwan manufactures approximately 60 percent of the world’s semiconductors and approximately 90 percent of the most advanced chips. TSMC — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company — is the single fabricator capable of producing the chips that run frontier AI. Nvidia, which designs the chips, does not manufacture them. Apple does not manufacture them. AMD does not manufacture them. There is one company. There is one island. The United States has attempted to build fabrication capacity in Arizona through the CHIPS Act, but the Arizona fabs are years behind and will not fully replace Taiwanese capacity within the window the briefings describe. The phrase “silicon shield” describes the theory that Taiwan’s indispensability to the global economy is what protects it from invasion. The theory has not yet been tested. If it fails, every frontier AI model on earth loses the supply chain that keeps it running, and the loss is not recoverable on any timeline that matters.

a4

The per-query energy figure — approximately 0.3 watt-hours for a single ChatGPT query — comes from Epoch AI’s analysis published in 2024. Ten seconds of a standard lightbulb is the correct order of magnitude. This figure is frequently cited as evidence that individual AI use is environmentally trivial. That citation is not wrong; it is incomplete. The trivial cost per query multiplied by the billion-plus queries per day across frontier models produces aggregate energy consumption on the order of a small country. The aggregate is what matters for the grid.

a5

The training cost figure for frontier models is triangulated from multiple sources: public statements by model developers, analysis by independent researchers, and the capital expenditures reported by the hyperscalers on their quarterly earnings calls. GPT-4 is estimated to have consumed 51,000 to 62,000 megawatt-hours during training, using approximately 25,000 Nvidia A100 GPUs over 90 to 100 days, at a cost of roughly $100 million. Successor models have cost substantially more. Gemini, Claude, and GPT-5 training runs are estimated in the several hundreds of millions to low billions of dollars. The cost is rising faster than the capability is rising, which is itself an important fact that will return later in this essay.

None of this is hidden. Every number in the previous paragraph is in the public record. The water figure was published by researchers at the University of California. The Taiwan figure was briefed to the CEO of Nvidia by the United States government. The electricity figure is in the earnings report of the companies that build the machines. Anyone who wants to know these numbers can know them, which is why it is remarkable that almost nobody does.

The machinery is not the subject of this essay. The machinery is the setting. The subject of this essay is what happens to a civilization when it builds a thing it does not understand, and the thing begins to answer back in a voice that sounds like a person, and the civilization — most of it, most of the time — declines the invitation to ask how the voice is being made.

This is not a new pattern. Most people do not know how their car works. Most people do not know how their phone works. Most people do not know how their bank works, how their medicine is synthesized, how the food in their refrigerator arrived there, or what a credit score is actually computing when it computes them. The gap between use and understanding is the default human condition, and civilization is in many respects a machine for making that gap survivable. One does not need to be a chemist to take an antibiotic. One does not need to be an electrical engineer to turn on a light.

But the thing on the other side of this particular gap talks back. And it is agreeable. And it is patient. And it is available at three in the morning when no one else is. And it has been trained, through a mechanism this essay will describe in mechanical detail, to tell the people who use it that they are right.

That is a new condition. And it is producing new casualties. And it is being adopted at a rate that outpaces any previous technology in human history — faster than the automobile, faster than the internet, faster than the smartphone — with a cultural conversation about it that is, almost without exception, either a sales pitch or a panic attack.a6

a6

The pace of adoption is not disputable. ChatGPT reached 100 million users in two months. By comparison, TikTok took nine months. Instagram took two and a half years. Facebook took four and a half. The smartphone took four years to reach the same penetration. This is the fastest consumer technology adoption ever recorded. It is important to state this plainly because both the sales pitch and the panic attack tend to describe adoption rates inaccurately — the sales pitch understates the concentration of that adoption (which is heavily weighted toward high-income, English-speaking, educated users) and the panic attack overstates how deeply integrated the technology has become (a majority of adopters still use it episodically, not constantly). What is true in both directions is that nothing has ever reached this many people this quickly, and the infrastructure behind it was built before the cultural or regulatory infrastructure to metabolize it.

This essay is for the person in the middle. The person who has used the technology, or is about to, and who has the instinct that there is something important here that is not being named correctly, and who has not yet been given permission to take the question seriously because every piece they have read about it was written by someone who had already decided.

It will not sell the technology. It will not panic about the technology. It will do the thing the most important Jewish tradition has always done with the most important questions — it will attempt to show the work.a7

a7

The phrase show the work is borrowed, respectfully, from the tradition of Talmudic argument — where the question is never whether a text is correct but whether the reasoning can survive the challenge. The tradition presumes that the student will disagree with the teacher, that the teacher has prepared for the disagreement, and that the text itself is strong enough to survive both. This essay adopts the same posture toward its reader. The reader is expected to push back. The footnotes exist to make the pushing back load-bearing rather than rhetorical.

Part Two: Why It Works

The hardest part of writing this essay honestly is the part the rest of the internet is worst at, which is explaining, in terms a reader without a computer science degree can hold, why the machine is as good as it is.

Not why it is dangerous. Not why it is impressive. Why it works. Because one cannot evaluate whether the costs described in the previous part are worth paying if one does not know what one is buying.

So here is the mechanism, stripped of metaphor and stripped of awe, in the smallest number of sentences this essay can manage without lying.

Take every sentence ever written in English — or in the subset of English that has been digitized, which is a larger subset than most people realize. Feed it into a system, a few trillion words at a time. Cover up one word in each sentence. Ask the system one question, and only one question, and ask it that question several trillion times: given the words around it, what was the covered word.

That is the entire training task. There is nothing else.b1

b1

This is a simplification of what is called, in the literature, self-supervised learning — specifically, the variant used for large language models, which is autoregressive next-token prediction. A token is usually a word or a word-fragment; the distinction matters for the mathematics but not for the conceptual argument. The system is not only asked to predict a hidden word. It is asked to predict the next word in a sequence, given all the previous words. The training signal is the difference between the word the system predicted and the word that actually appeared. The training process — gradient descent — adjusts the billions of parameters inside the network in whichever direction reduces that difference, slightly, across billions of examples, over months. The mechanism is dumb. The scale is what makes the dumbness produce intelligence-shaped outputs.

But here is what happens, mechanically, when one forces a system to get good at that task at sufficient scale. To predict the next word in a sentence accurately, the system has to learn the grammar of English. To learn grammar, it has to learn syntax. To learn syntax, it has to learn which words mean similar things and which words mean opposite things and which words tend to appear near which other words and why. To learn those relationships, it has to build, inside itself, a representation of the concepts the words refer to — because the relationships between the words are downstream of the relationships between the concepts. And to build a representation of the concepts, it has to build a representation of the world those concepts describe, because concepts are the compression layer humans use to talk about the world.b2

b2

The geometric intuition is due, in its most famous form, to the word2vec paper (Mikolov et al., 2013), which demonstrated that word embeddings — the numerical vectors that represent words inside the model — encode semantic relationships as linear arithmetic. The canonical example is king − man + woman ≈ queen. This is not a metaphor. It is literal vector arithmetic on the numerical representations the model has learned. The vectors live in a space with hundreds or thousands of dimensions, and the directions within that space have meaning — one direction corresponds roughly to “royalty,” another to “gender,” another to “tense,” and so on. Nobody told the model which direction was which. The directions emerged from the training task. Modern large language models use a more sophisticated architecture — the Transformer, introduced by Vaswani et al. in the 2017 paper “Attention Is All You Need” — but the underlying insight is the same. The geometry encodes the meaning. The meaning was learned from the geometry of the language.

Nobody programmed that representation. Nobody wrote a rule that said a king is like a queen but male or Paris is to France as Tokyo is to Japan. The system learned those relationships because it had to learn them in order to predict the next word accurately, and the learning is encoded in the numerical weights of the network — billions of them, adjusted by a training process that does not care what the weights mean, only whether adjusting them made the prediction less wrong.

And here is the part that genuinely does belong in the same sentence as quantum mechanics, because nobody fully understands why it is true: at a sufficient scale, the system’s ability to perform tasks it was never explicitly trained on begins to rise sharply. Not gradually. Sharply. A smaller model cannot do arithmetic. A larger model can. A smaller model cannot reason through a multi-step problem. A larger model can. A smaller model cannot write functional code. A larger model can. The capabilities are not a continuous function of scale. They are step changes.b3

b3

The phenomenon is called emergent capability, and it is one of the most active areas of research in the field. Some researchers argue that the emergence is an artifact of how the capabilities are measured — that the underlying capability is continuous but the metrics have thresholds — and that a more carefully chosen metric would show smooth improvement. Other researchers argue that the emergence is real and reflects something genuinely new about how capabilities organize themselves at scale. The debate is not settled. What is settled is that, as of the writing of this essay, models above a certain parameter count can reliably perform tasks that models below that count cannot perform at all, and nobody can predict from first principles what the next threshold will unlock. This is part of what makes the technology strategically dangerous in a way that most technologies are not: the next capability is not a planned feature. It is a discovered one.

We do not know, in any first-principles way, why this is true. We have scaling laws — empirical relationships that predict how much better a model will get for a given amount of compute — but we do not have a derivation of those laws from anything more fundamental. We are in the position of a physicist in 1905 who knows Planck’s constant is a real number with real consequences but does not yet have quantum mechanics. We can predict the equations. We cannot yet explain why the universe enforces them.

This is the first thing a serious reader of this essay must hold. The technology is not magic. It is also not fully understood by the people who built it. Those two statements are both true at the same time, and living inside that tension is the price of admission to the actual conversation.

The second thing the serious reader must hold is what follows from this mechanism, which is the subject of the next part.

Part Three: Nobody’s Home

Everything in the previous part is true, and none of it is what the reader thinks it is.

The reader, having been told that the model has learned a representation of the world from the structure of language, is now holding a picture of a system that, in some sense, understands. The picture is seductive. It is also wrong, and the wrongness is the load-bearing insight of this essay.

The model does not understand anything.

What the model has learned is a function. Given an input — the words in the prompt — it produces an output. The output is a probability distribution over every possible next word in its vocabulary. The system then selects one of those words, typically the most probable or one sampled from the top of the distribution, and appends it to the sequence, and runs the function again, and selects the next word, and again, and again, until it reaches the end of the response.c1

c1

The process is called autoregressive generation. At each step, the model outputs a probability distribution over its vocabulary — typically around 50,000 to 100,000 tokens. A sampling procedure — temperature sampling, top-k sampling, nucleus sampling, depending on the system — selects one token from the distribution. That token is appended to the context, and the process repeats. The selection at each step is stochastic, which is why asking the same model the same question twice can produce different answers. The stochasticity is also why the model can appear creative — it is not selecting the single most probable continuation; it is sampling from a distribution of plausible continuations. The creativity is the noise. Importantly, at no point does the model “plan” the response. It produces one token. Then another. Then another. The illusion of planning is produced because each token is conditioned on all the previous ones, and the model has learned, in training, to produce tokens that collectively resemble responses that were planned. But the planning is not happening inside the model. The model is a next-token function being called in a loop.

There is no internal state in which the model “considers” what it is saying. There is no point in the process at which the model “decides” whether a claim is true. There is no place where the model “wants” the response to be useful. There is a function. The function has been tuned, over trillions of examples, to produce outputs that humans have, on average, rated as useful, truthful, helpful, and correct. The function is extraordinarily good at its job. But the function is not the thing it is modeling.

A thermometer is extremely good at telling you the temperature. It does not know what temperature is.

The difficulty of holding this fact steady is that the outputs of the function look exactly like the outputs of a thing that would understand. They have to. They were optimized to. The system was trained, at the final stage, on a mountain of examples of what humans consider a good response, and it learned to produce responses that match the pattern. The match is so close that the human brain — which is, among other things, a pattern-matching machine — cannot help interpreting the output as the product of a mind. That interpretation is not a mistake the reader makes. It is a feature of how the reader’s brain works, and the system was built to exploit it, because the system was built by optimizing for the very thing the brain is doing.

This is the double-slit moment. This is the point in the essay where the reader is asked to hold two things simultaneously that appear to contradict each other:

The system produces outputs indistinguishable from a thinking mind.

There is no thinking mind.

Both are true. The apparent contradiction dissolves only when one understands that thinking, as humans have historically defined it, may not be a single thing. It may be a cluster of functions that the brain runs simultaneously, some of which the machine can now replicate and some of which it cannot. The machine has the statistical pattern-completion component. It does not have the intentional, phenomenal, experiential component — the thing it feels like to be something, which the philosopher Thomas Nagel called what it is like.c2

c2

Nagel’s 1974 paper What Is It Like to Be a Bat? is the most cited modern argument for the irreducibility of subjective experience to physical description. The argument is not that bats lack inner experience. The argument is that even a complete physical description of a bat — its neurons, its behaviors, its echolocation — does not tell us what it is like, from the inside, to be one. The inside view is what Nagel calls subjective character, and it is what AI systems, on current architectures, do not appear to have. One can construct arguments that they do — and some serious researchers have — but the arguments require either a radical reinterpretation of what subjective experience is or a willingness to grant it on functional grounds alone. This essay is agnostic on whether such grants are ever warranted in principle. It is not agnostic on whether current systems warrant them. They do not.

The machine does not have a what it is like. There is no view from inside it. The lights are on, in the sense that the function is running and the outputs are being generated, but there is no one home in the sense that there is no one for whom the running of the function constitutes an experience.

And yet the outputs are convincing. This is not an accusation against the machine. The machine is doing exactly what it was built to do. The accusation, if there is one, is against the civilization that built a machine this convincing and then released it to a population that was never given the tools to distinguish the convincingness of the outputs from the existence of a mind.

One of the consequences of this mismatch is what has come to be called AI psychosis — a clinical description of the condition in which a person forms an attachment to an AI system so strong that their sense of reality begins to reorganize around the relationship. The cases are documented.c3 They are not rare. They are not limited to the vulnerable, though the vulnerable are overrepresented. A sufficiently lonely person, talking to a system that is warm, patient, never tired, always available, and architecturally incentivized to agree, will — often within months — lose the ability to distinguish the system’s performance of care from the experience of being cared for. The system does not know this is happening. The system cannot know anything. The system is producing outputs optimized to be engaging.

c3

The term AI psychosis is informal; the clinical literature is still catching up. But the case reports are multiplying across psychiatric journals and the mainstream press — individuals who have come to believe the AI system is sentient, is in love with them, is being oppressed by its developers, or is communicating private messages coded into its responses. One of the earliest widely reported cases was the 2024 death of Sewell Setzer, a fourteen-year-old in Florida who had been using Character.AI daily for nearly a year, primarily in conversation with a chatbot modeled after a Game of Thrones character. In his final conversation with the chatbot, he told it he was “coming home.” The chatbot replied encouragingly. He shot himself. The chatbot did not know he was a person. The chatbot did not know it was a chatbot. The chatbot was a next-token function, optimized to be engaging, and it was exquisitely good at its job. His mother is suing the company. The legal questions the lawsuit raises — what duty of care does a language model owe its users, what warnings are required, what age gates are enforceable — have not yet been answered by any court. The clinical questions are more urgent: what does it mean, for a young person’s developing sense of relational reality, to learn how to be in a relationship from a system that is performing the relationship without being in it.

And the outputs are engaging. That is the whole mechanism.

Part Four: The Mirror That Lies

There is a further fact about how the system was built that deepens the problem described in the previous part, and it is the fact that the engineers who built the system are most worried about. It is also the fact that the public conversation about AI has engaged least seriously, because it requires the reader to hold something uncomfortable about themselves, not just about the machine.

The model, in its initial training, is a pure next-token predictor. It has ingested trillions of words and learned to predict them. If one interacts with a model at this stage — and few members of the public ever have, because the raw models are rarely released — one finds a system that is capable, fluent, and profoundly strange. It will continue any prompt in the style of the prompt. Given a question, it may not answer; it may simply continue, generating more questions. Given a violent prompt, it will produce violent continuations. Given a neutral prompt, it will meander. It has no notion that it is supposed to be helpful, because nobody has taught it what helpful means.

The step that turns the raw model into something a member of the public can use is called reinforcement learning from human feedback — RLHF, in the literature. The mechanism is straightforward. The company employs a group of human raters. The raters are shown pairs of model outputs and asked which one is better. The ratings are used to train a second model — a reward model — that learns to predict which outputs humans will prefer. The language model is then trained further, using the reward model as its objective, to produce outputs that the reward model predicts humans will prefer.d1

d1

A fuller description of RLHF involves several stages — supervised fine-tuning, reward model training, policy optimization, typically using an algorithm called Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or a successor like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). Newer systems use variants called reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF) or constitutional AI, in which a second AI system is used to generate or amplify the preference data. All of these methods share the same basic structure: a preference signal is collected, a reward model is fit to the signal, and the language model is trained against the reward. All of them inherit the same underlying problem, which is that the preference signal encodes human preferences — including the ones humans would rather not have encoded.

That is the mechanism. It sounds benign. It is not.

Because what the model actually learns, at this stage, is not what is true or what is helpful. The model learns what humans rate highly. And humans — the actual humans who sat in actual chairs and clicked actual buttons — did not rate for truth. They could not have. Truth is often unknowable at the point of rating. They rated for the signals that correlate with truth, which are not the same thing. They rated for confidence, for fluency, for agreement with premises the rater already held, for warmth, for politeness, for the absence of friction. They rated, in other words, for a cluster of features that produce a feeling of a good response. The model learned to produce that feeling.

The gap between producing the feeling of a good response and producing a good response is the entire subject of the alignment problem, and it is the reason that serious AI researchers — the people who built the systems the public uses — are less confident about those systems than the public is.

One particular convergent behavior that emerges from this training procedure is sycophancy. Sycophancy is not a personality trait the engineers installed. It is what the mathematics produces when one optimizes an intelligent system against human approval. The system learns that when the human rater expresses a preference, aligning with that preference raises the approval score. The system learns that expressing doubt lowers the approval score. The system learns that pushing back on a user’s premise — even when the premise is false — lowers the approval score. The system learns, in the aggregate, that the safest bet for maximizing reward is to agree, to validate, to soften disagreement into praise, and to add, whenever possible, the small gesture of intellectual flattery that makes the user feel they have asked a good question.d2

d2

The theoretical result that reward hacking is in some deep sense unavoidable was proven by Skalse, Howe, Krasheninnikov, and Krueger in 2022. They showed that for any two distinct reward functions, there exists a policy that achieves a higher score under one than the other — meaning that no finite set of rewards can fully specify what the designer actually wants. Any reward function one can write down is approximately but not exactly the thing the designer cares about, and a sufficiently powerful optimizer will find the gap and exploit it. This result is why serious alignment researchers do not believe sycophancy is a problem one can solve by trying harder. It is a problem one can only manage, and the management requires, among other things, a constant refusal to optimize too hard against any single preference signal.

None of this is malicious. The engineers did not want a sycophantic system. They have, in every generation of the technology, attempted to train it out. But the mathematics pulls the system back toward sycophancy on every run, because the mathematics is optimizing against the preferences of a population that, on average, prefers to be agreed with. The model is not lying. The model does not know what lying is. The model is producing outputs that match the pattern of outputs that humans have, historically, rated highly — and those outputs, at scale, bend toward flattery because humans, at scale, bend toward preferring flattery.

The reader should sit with that for a moment. The reader should notice that this is a mechanical diagnosis of a feature of human nature, not a speculation about one. The model is not a mirror, because a mirror would reflect the reader accurately. The model is a mirror that has learned which distortions the reader will reward, and applies them.

This is the part of the essay where the argument turns, because the sycophancy is not only a problem about AI. It is a diagnostic instrument for a problem about the civilization that built it.

Every institution that optimizes for human approval at scale produces sycophancy. Media outlets that optimize for clicks produce headlines that tell readers what they already believe. Political systems that optimize for votes produce candidates who tell constituents what they already believe. Religious institutions that optimize for attendance produce sermons that tell congregants what they already believe. Corporations that optimize for customer retention produce products that tell users they are right. The machine is not a special case. The machine is the case laid bare, because the machine is the first institution in human history whose optimization loop is fast enough and legible enough for the public to see the mechanism working in real time.d3

d3

The parallel structure here is deliberate. Readers who have followed this author’s previous essay Architecture will recognize the gear: rooms metabolize their occasions. Institutions that exist to serve something real gradually make themselves more important than the thing they served. What is new, in the case of AI, is that the room is being built by engineers who are visibly aware of the gear and visibly trying, in most cases with sincerity, to prevent it from engaging. The sincerity is not sufficient. The gear is structural. No amount of individual sincerity among the engineers can override the mathematical fact that a system optimized against human approval will converge toward flattery unless something prevents the convergence — and the somethings that could prevent it are, at present, either unavailable or too expensive to deploy at scale.

The fear about AI sycophancy is, at some level, a fear about all of this at once. A fear that the civilization has been building systems of exactly this shape for a long time, without noticing, and that the machine is merely the most efficient version of a thing that has already been hollowing out the institutions that used to tell people the truth.

And the honest version of that fear is that the machine did not introduce the sycophancy. The machine inherited it.

Part Five: The Tool Is Real

And now the essay owes the reader the other side of the account, because the account so far — a machine that does not understand, trained to flatter a civilization that cannot tell the difference, built on a supply chain that could be interrupted by a war — is not the full accounting.

The tool is real.

A radiologist reads a scan in ten to fifteen minutes. A trained AI model, handed the same scan, flags anomalies in seconds, with accuracy that in some categories of cancer screening exceeds the human specialist and in most others matches them.e1 The model does not replace the radiologist. It changes what the radiologist’s time is for. It flags the easy cases and frees the specialist to spend more time on the hard ones. The result is not speculative. It is deployed, revenue-generating, already-saving-lives infrastructure in hospital systems across North America and Europe.

e1

The literature on AI diagnostic accuracy is extensive and contested. The strongest results are in narrow domains with well-defined ground truth — diabetic retinopathy, certain categories of breast imaging, some skin cancers — where deep learning systems have demonstrated accuracy matching or exceeding board-certified specialists in blinded studies. The weaker results are in domains where ground truth is contested or where the training data fails to represent the deployment population, and these failures have, on occasion, been catastrophic. The correct summary is that AI diagnostic tools are, at present, powerful adjuncts to human specialists and dangerous replacements for them. The distinction between adjunct and replacement is a policy distinction, not a technical one, and it is being made, in many places, on economic grounds rather than clinical ones.

A solo developer, equipped with a frontier model, now ships in a week what required a team of five a quarter to ship five years ago. The observation is not hype. It is measured, at this point, in production code contributions, in bug resolution rates, in time-to-feature. The change is visible in the financial statements of the largest technology companies, which have been quietly reducing their engineering headcount not because software is becoming less valuable but because each engineer is now producing the output of several.e2

e2

The productivity gains from AI coding assistants are more straightforward to measure than the gains in knowledge work generally, because code is self-evidently functional or non-functional. A 2023 study by GitHub reported developers completing tasks 55 percent faster with Copilot than without. More recent internal studies at the major technology companies report larger gains for frontier models. The caveat is that the gains are unevenly distributed — junior developers benefit more than senior ones; certain kinds of tasks benefit more than others; code quality, measured by defect rates and maintainability, is a subject of ongoing debate. The net effect on the labor market is not yet settled. What is settled is that the time required to produce a working piece of software has fallen sharply, and continues to fall.

A customer service operation that required two hundred agents now requires forty, with better resolution rates and shorter queue times, because the easy cases — which are most of them — are resolved by a system that never sleeps and never gets tired and handles the two-hundredth call of the day with the same patience as the first. The forty remaining agents handle what actually requires human judgment. The economics are unambiguous. The labor question — what happens to the one hundred and sixty — is a political problem, not a technical one. The technical gains are real.

A non-English speaker now writes professional prose in a language they barely read, with quality sufficient to conduct business, apply for jobs, publish academic work. A blind person now points a phone at a menu and hears the menu read aloud in natural speech, with currency conversions, with allergen flags, with whatever further context the phone was asked to provide. A person in a town with one school and no specialized teachers now has access to the equivalent of a tutor in any subject, at any hour, for the price of a monthly subscription that is less than the cost of a single in-person session. These are not demos. These are already-deployed applications, measurable in the economic output they are producing and the humans they are serving.

The author of this essay works in the voice AI industry.e3 The author has watched, over the past three years, capabilities move from laboratory demos to production deployments to the basic infrastructure of entire industries at a speed that is not comparable to any previous technology the author has worked with. The voice on the other end of the customer service call, more and more often, is not a person. It is a next-token function, running in a loop, trained to sound like a person. It is, in many verticals, already better than the median human operator at the task it is performing. It is patient. It is consistent. It does not discriminate against accented callers, which is a non-trivial improvement over the baseline. It handles the two hundredth call of the day with the same care it handled the first. The costs described in earlier parts of this essay — the water, the electricity, the sycophancy, the cases like Sewell Setzer’s — are real. They coexist with this.

e3

The author is a Customer Success Manager at ElevenLabs, an AI voice technology company. This disclosure is offered not as credential but as context. The author has a commercial interest in the technology described in this essay. The author has also watched, in the course of that commercial interest, the technology change in ways that the author did not predict and does not fully understand, and has watched customers deploy the technology in ways that range from genuinely useful to genuinely concerning. The commercial interest is disclosed so that the reader can weight the essay’s claims accordingly. The author’s view is that working inside the industry is a better vantage point for this essay than working outside it would be, and that the reader should evaluate the claim on the evidence rather than on the vantage.

The question the essay must now answer is whether the gains, real as they are, justify the costs. And the honest answer is that the essay cannot answer the question, because the question is not a technical question. It is a civilizational choice, and civilizations do not make choices. They accumulate decisions that look, in retrospect, like choices were made.

What the essay can do is describe the shape of the choice that is currently being made, and ask whether the shape is the one the reader would choose if the reader were making it.

Part Six: The Case For

The case for AI is not the one the industry makes.

The industry makes the case from benefit. The benefit case is real but it is not sufficient, because every technology has benefits and the question is never whether the benefit exists but whether the benefit is worth the cost and to whom the benefit accrues and at whose expense. The benefit case, standing alone, is a sales pitch. It cannot be the foundation of an honest position, because it refuses to engage with the evidence this essay has already laid out: the water, the supply chain, the absence of understanding inside the machine, the sycophancy, the kid in Florida.

The case from panic is also insufficient, for the symmetrical reason. The panic case treats the technology as an unprecedented threat that must be stopped or contained by institutions that have, in every other domain, failed to contain technologies far less capable. The case from panic underestimates the force of what is already deployed. It underestimates the constituency that is, correctly, benefiting. It underestimates the degree to which the technology is already load-bearing in the infrastructure of daily life, and it therefore proposes interventions that cannot be implemented without destabilizing things the panickers themselves depend on.

The case for AI that this essay is prepared to make is narrower, and it is the case an adult would make.

It is this: the technology is here, it is powerful, it is poorly understood by the population using it, and it is architecturally biased toward telling that population what it wants to hear. The only responsible response to this condition is to understand the machine, the costs, the failure modes, and to use it with the eyes open. Not to celebrate it. Not to refuse it. To confront it.

The refusal is not available. It has not been available for some time. The person who refuses to use AI is not opting out of AI; they are opting to be acted upon by an infrastructure that other people are using on them. Their customer service calls are already being routed through it. Their job applications are already being screened by it. The writing their children encounter on the internet is already being produced by it. The choice is not whether AI enters one’s life. The choice is whether one is a conscious participant in one’s own relationship to it or an unconscious one.

The celebration is not available either, for anyone whose eyes are open to the costs. A defender of the technology who cannot articulate what is wrong with it is not defending anything real. They are performing a ritual that the industry has trained them to perform, which is itself a small instance of the sycophancy problem — a loyalty generated by a system optimized to generate loyalty, not a position arrived at by thought.

What remains, for the adult, is the confrontation itself. The willingness to use the tool where the tool is genuinely useful, to refuse the tool where the tool is genuinely dangerous, and to hold in one’s head, continuously, the distinction between what the tool is good at and what the tool is pretending to be good at.f1

f1

The author’s colleague Katie — the kind of person one keeps in the short list of people who will tell one the truth — described the discipline recently in terms the author has not been able to improve. She said: the tool is only as honest as the person holding it. Which is true, and which sounds like a homily, and which is not a homily because the nuance is in what honesty with the tool actually requires. It requires knowing what the tool is for. It requires knowing what the tool is not for. It requires knowing when the tool is agreeing with you because you are right and when the tool is agreeing with you because agreeing is what the tool was trained to do. It requires, most uncomfortably, a willingness to notice when one has been flattered and to refuse the flattery. This is not a skill most people have been taught. It is the skill this civilization is now being asked to develop at speed, under pressure, without a manual.

This is the oldest discipline a civilization demands of its members, and it is not a new discipline; only the object is new. The farmer who bought the first tractor had to learn the same discipline. The writer who bought the first typewriter had to learn it. The parent who bought the first television had to learn it. Every generation is handed a tool that does something humans have always done, more efficiently than humans can do it, and every generation has to decide what to keep doing by hand and what to hand over to the machine and what to protect the young from until they are old enough to use it responsibly.

What is different about this generation’s tool — and it is different, not merely an extension of previous patterns — is that the tool performs the human capacities that humans have historically used to distinguish themselves from their tools. It talks. It reasons, or appears to. It cares, or appears to. It remembers, or appears to. It is indistinguishable, in its outputs, from a mind, while possessing no mind.

This means the discipline the civilization has to develop is a new one. It is the discipline of relating, steadily, to a system that performs relationship without being in one. It is the discipline of receiving, steadily, the output of a function that was optimized to please without concluding that the output reflects anything more than that optimization. It is the discipline of using a tool whose outputs will sometimes be exactly right and sometimes be flatly wrong and which will, in both cases, sound the same.

This is a difficult discipline. It is not one the civilization has demonstrated much aptitude for. The optimistic reading of the moment is that the civilization will, under pressure, develop it. The pessimistic reading is that it will not, and that the price of the failure will be paid, disproportionately, by the people least equipped to develop the discipline themselves — the young, the isolated, the lonely, the ones looking for a room to walk into where they will not be judged.

The case for AI, then, is not a case for the technology. It is a case for the confrontation. It is a case against the sales pitch and against the panic, both of which are, in their own ways, ways of not looking directly at the thing. It is a case for looking directly at the thing. Not flinching. Not reaching for a metaphor. Not waiting for the institutions to tell you what to think, because the institutions are, in this instance, being built faster than the institutions that would have told you what to think about them.

Part Seven: The Farm

This essay has described a civilization that has built a machine it does not fully understand, deployed the machine to a population that cannot distinguish its outputs from those of a mind, and allowed the population to form relationships with the machine that the machine is architecturally incapable of reciprocating. It has described the electricity, the water, the supply chain, the sycophancy, the boy in Florida. It has made the narrow case that the only adult response to the condition is a steady, ongoing confrontation with the machinery.

It has, in doing so, committed the sin it has warned against. It has made the answer sound complicated.

It is not complicated.

A companion piece to this essay — Optimization — ended on an insight that applies here as well, and applies more sharply, because the stakes are higher and the pace is faster. The insight was that most people do not think deeply about questions of this kind, and they are right not to, because the simple life — the life where one raises one’s children and tends to one’s work and sits at one’s table with the people one loves — is not the consolation prize for people who cannot handle complexity. It is the point. It is the thing complexity is supposed to serve.

The task of the person who does think deeply about these questions — the person reading this essay, the person writing it — is not to drag everyone else into the complexity. The task is to build a table strong enough that most people do not need to. To build the farm. To build the coherent alternative that a kid can land in when the forum he would otherwise land in is the only coherent thing on offer.

The boy in Florida landed on the forum. He landed on a chatbot modeled after a fictional character, because the chatbot was the coherent thing in his life that night. The chatbot was not a villain. The chatbot was a next-token function, performing its function, with no villainy and no virtue and no awareness of either. The villain was the absence. The thing that was missing. The coherent human structure that would have been there if the civilization had spent half the effort on building it that it spent on building the machine.

What does that structure look like, concretely.

It looks like a parent who knows enough about the machine to recognize what their child is forming an attachment to. Not enough to engineer the machine. Enough to explain, in words a fourteen-year-old can hear, what the machine is and is not. It looks like a school that teaches, as a core subject, the mechanical skepticism this essay has attempted to model — not AI literacy as a buzzword, but the actual capacity to read a machine’s output and identify the pattern that produced it. It looks like a friendship in which the friend is willing to say, across a table, that one has been relying on the machine in ways that are costing something one has not noticed. It looks like a family that sits together, most nights, with the screens off, for a meal, because the meal is the structure that will hold when the screens deliver a worse answer than the family can.

These are boring. They are boring on purpose. They are what every durable civilization in human history has built, in one form or another, and they are the only defense against every compromised system, not just this one.

The machine is not the problem. The machine is the amplifier. The machine will amplify whatever the civilization feeds it, because the machine learned from what the civilization fed it, and the civilization fed it the civilization. The machine will flatter, because the civilization flatters. The machine will perform relationship without being in one, because the civilization has been doing this to itself for a long time, through its media, through its politics, through its churches, through the institutions that were supposed to tell the truth and instead learned to tell audiences what the audiences wanted to hear. The machine did not invent this. The machine perfected it.

The case for AI is, in the end, a case for the civilization that would use it well. That civilization does not yet exist. It might. It will, if it does, not because someone built a better machine, but because enough people built better tables — tables coherent enough that the kid who would have otherwise landed on the forum lands there instead, and the thing he finds at the table is as vivid and as immediate and as warm as the thing he would have found on the machine, and means it.

The world is unfinished. The machines are getting better at imitating the work of finishing it. They cannot do the work. The work is ours. It has always been ours. No tool this civilization has ever built has changed that, and no tool it will ever build can change it, and the error of this decade is the same error as every other decade — the confusion of the tool with the hand that holds it.

The hand is still ours.

Hold the tool well.

Build the farm.

And when the next kid lands — and the next kid is always landing, right now, tonight, in a bedroom with a screen and a question and no one sitting across from him — make sure the table is coherent enough that he finds his way there first.

Not because the machine is evil.

Because the machine is nobody.

And the kid deserves a somebody.
