Part One: The Collarbone

There is a young man who made himself infertile for the width of his collarbones.a1

a1

The condition is self-reported but consistent across multiple public interviews. The young man has stated that years of anabolic steroid use beginning at age fourteen caused his body to stop producing testosterone naturally, rendering him infertile. He has described himself as “a lab rat of the community” — referring to the movement for maximizing physical appearance — “willing to try anything in order to ascend.” The word ascend is not incidental. It is the movement’s term for becoming more physically attractive and thereby gaining social power and sexual access. The religious connotation is not accidental either, though it is unlikely anyone in the movement would describe it that way.

His name, for the purposes of this essay, matters less than his archetype — though his name is a matter of public record and his story has been profiled in newspapers of record, glossy magazines, and broadcast interviews watched by millions.a2 He was born at the turn of the twenty-first century in the American Northeast. His father was a businessman. His mother stayed home. He attended one of the oldest Catholic college preparatory schools in his state — an institution founded before the Civil War, administered by priests, whose stated mission was “the development of the whole man in the values of the Catholic faith: spiritually, intellectually, socially, and physically.”a3

a2

His legal name is Braden Eric Peters, born December 17, 2005, in Hoboken, New Jersey. He has been profiled by The New York Times and GQ, interviewed by Piers Morgan on international television, and covered extensively by digital media. His online alias, Clavicular, derives from the clavicle bone. These details are documented in his Wikipedia biography and in a Cosmopolitan profile, among other sources.

a3

Seton Hall Preparatory School, founded 1856, is the oldest Catholic college preparatory school in New Jersey. It is administered by priests of the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Newark. The mission statement — “the development of the whole man in the values of the Catholic faith: spiritually, intellectually, socially, and physically” — is posted on the school’s website and articulated by the school’s president. The phrase the whole man will return several times in this essay. Source

At fourteen, during a period of mass isolation brought on by a global pandemic, the boy was online fourteen hours a day. He was browsing forums where men rated each other’s faces and prescribed regimens for physical transformation. He was skipping the virtual classes his school delivered through a screen. He ordered testosterone off the internet and began injecting himself in his bedroom. When his parents found the vials, they sent him to live with his grandmother. He kept going.a4

Skipping virtual classes his Catholic school was delivering during pandemic lockdowns. He has said he hid testosterone vials from his parents and that when caught, he was sent to his grandmother’s. He enrolled at Sacred Heart University in the fall of 2024 and was expelled three weeks later for hiding testosterone in his dorm room — according to Peters, the result of forum users reporting him to the school’s administration. He has stated that he is on the autism spectrum, though he has not been professionally diagnosed. This detail is included not to pathologize him but because he offers it himself as part of his self-understanding, and because the question of what systems owe to the people most vulnerable to their incentive structures is central to what this essay is about. He had been interviewed on international broadcast television. He walked a runway during fashion week. He was selling a monthly subscription course to teenage boys on how to “ascend.” He had allegedly used hard stimulants to suppress his appetite. He had injected a seventeen-year-old girl with fat-dissolving peptides on camera, in front of thousands of viewers. He calls himself by a name derived from the clavicle — the collarbone — because in the world he inhabits, clavicle width is one of the metrics by which a man’s value is measured.a5

This essay is not interested in making this young man a villain. He asked a question that every adolescent asks — who am I, what am I for, what makes me valuable — and the answer he received was the most coherent one available to him at the time.

What is interesting is why nobody else answered it first.

Because here is the fact that is never discussed when this young man’s story is discussed. The institution that educated him — a Catholic school whose explicit, stated, centuries-old mission is the formation of the whole man — produced a student who concluded that the answer to what am I for was the width of his collarbones.a6 The school did not fail him because it was negligent. The school failed him because it was incoherent. Its doctrine said one thing. Its behavior — a video link during a crisis, an institution that had long since stopped being able to explain itself in terms a fourteen-year-old could feel in his bones — said another. (We will get to this dissonance later in the essay.) And in the gap between doctrine and behavior, a website walked in with an answer that was, whatever else one might say about it, internally consistent.

a6

This is not an attack on any specific institution. The point is structural, not personal. A Catholic institution whose mission is the formation of the whole man existed in this boy’s life during the precise years when the question what am I for was most urgent — and the answer he received from the institution was less coherent, less immediate, and less actionable than the answer he received from a website. That is a civilizational failure, not an individual one.

The movement that captured him told him what to optimize for. It told him how. It told him what the metrics were. It rewarded him when he hit them. It punished him — through rating systems, through forums, through the brutal social hierarchy of the community — when he did not. The system was precise. The system was measurable. The system was immediate.

The Catholic school’s answer to the same question was two thousand years old and delivered through a screen.

The boy went where the coherence was.

This essay is about coherence — what it is, how it builds power, and why the most dangerous moment in a civilization’s life is the one where the old coherences fail and the vacuum fills with whatever system moves fastest. It is a companion in spirit to another essay of mine called “Architecture,” which asked what institutions are actually for and who they actually serve. This one asks a different question — the question that boy was asking at fourteen, which is the same question any reader is asking right now whether they would use these words for it or not:

What are you optimizing for?a7

a7

If one has read “The Architecture,” one knows this question is not asked innocently. Every question is a room. This one is too. The essay that follows metabolizes religion, power, a young man’s infertility, and the concept of civilization into something that can be read on a screen.

Peters has been called “representative of the generation of permanent adolescents” by one publication, “the new Andrew Tate” by another. Thomas Chatterton Williams described the movement as “decadence without pleasure, cruelty without purpose, vanity stripped of even the dignity of irony.” Each of these framings is partially right. None of them asks the question this essay is interested in, which is not what is wrong with the movement but what question is the movement answering that nobody else bothered to answer first.

Part Two: The Mechanism

The question what are you optimizing for is not a modern question.b1 It is the oldest question any civilization can ask, and the history of power is the history of which systems answered it most coherently.

b1

The question of what a civilization optimizes for is not typically framed this way in academic literature, but the concept is central to the work of Joseph Henrich (The WEIRDest People in the World, 2020), which traces how specific cultural practices — not genes, not geography — produced the psychological profiles that shaped Western institutions. Henrich’s argument is structural: the Catholic Church’s medieval marriage policies inadvertently produced a population of individualists who organized around voluntary associations rather than kin networks. The optimization target changed, and the civilization that emerged was a product of that change. What a system optimizes for determines what the system becomes. This is not philosophy. It is engineering.

Not most correctly. Most coherently. The distinction matters more than anything else in this essay.

A system can be wrong about everything and still be coherent — still have its doctrine aligned with its behavior, its promises matched by its demands, its internal logic holding up under the weight of the lives it organizes. A system can be right about everything and still be incoherent — gaps between what it says and what it does, enough gray space in its doctrine that any leader with ambition can justify almost anything. The coherent system will produce more durable power. It will always produce more durable power. Not because coherence is virtue. Because coherence is architectural, a form of pattern recognition, and these patterns are what humans seem attracted to more than anything in systems of belief.b2

b2

This is the load-bearing distinction of the essay. Coherence, as used here, does not mean truth. It means internal alignment — the degree to which a system’s stated values, its behavioral demands, its reward structures, and its punishments all point in the same direction. A cult can be coherent. A terrorist organization can be coherent. Coherence is the precondition of durable power. Whether that power serves good or evil is a separate question — one we will return to, but not yet.

The clearest engineering diagrams available for how coherence builds power are religions.

Judaism is the precision model.b3

b3

The use of Judaism as an engineering case study will be read by some as antisemitism and by others as philo-Semitic romanticization. It is neither. This is a description of a structural feature of a system that has survived for thousands of years, asking what that structural feature produces. The scholarly literature is extensive and serious — Paul Johnson’s A History of the Jews (1987), Neal Gabler’s An Empire of Their Own (1988), Joseph Henrich’s cross-cultural work on how religious practices shape institutional behavior. The distance between scholarly observation and paranoid conspiracy is rigor, specificity, and the willingness to name complexity rather than flatten it.

(What follows will activate defenses the reader may not know they have, and those defenses are themselves part of the pattern being described. The conversation about Judaism and power is one of the most polluted conversations in human history — polluted by antisemitism, by conspiracy, by people who see the pattern and flatten it into something monstrous. This essay is not doing that. This essay is attempting to not only show the answer with a semblance of mathematical precision, but show the work on how it got here.)

Judaism, as a system, is internally coherent in a way that very few other systems have managed to sustain over millennia. Its doctrine is precise. Its demands are specific. Its conception of purpose — the thing it tells its adherents they are optimizing for — is not vague aspiration but actionable obligation. The Hebrew concept of tikkun olam — repairing the world — is not a metaphor. It is a job description.b4 The idea, rooted in the Torah and expanded through centuries of rabbinic and mystical tradition, is that creation was left intentionally unfinished. God did not build a complete world and hand it to humanity. God built an incomplete world and made humanity a partner in its completion. Rabbi Judah Loew of Prague, writing in the sixteenth century, put it this way: creation was left in “potential,” and it is incumbent upon humans to “actualize” through their intellect the work started during the six days of creation.

b4

The concept of tikkun olam — “repairing the world” — traces to the earliest layers of Jewish thought and has been elaborated across centuries of tradition. Rabbi Judah Loew of Prague (c. 1525–1609) wrote that creation was left in “potential,” and it is incumbent upon humans to “actualize” through intellect the work started during the six days of creation. The Lurianic Kabbalah of Rabbi Isaac Luria (1534–1572) developed the idea further: the world was not merely unfinished but actively broken — divine light shattered into sparks scattered through creation, and the human task is to gather and restore them. The Chabad tradition puts it plainly: “He made us His partners in the creation of heaven and earth. Now that changes everything.” Source Source

That is a fundamentally different answer to what are you optimizing for than anything the modern West has produced. The West says: innovate. Build. Disrupt. Move fast and break things. Judaism says: the world is already broken. Your job is to fix it. Not to build something new. To complete what was started.

The difference sounds academic until one watches what it produces over centuries. A system optimized for completion rather than expansion does not need majority adoption to be powerful. It can operate as a minority influence within larger systems because its coherence is internal — not dependent on controlling the external environment. It produces depth rather than breadth. It produces undercurrents — forces that reshape civilizations without conquering them, that accumulate influence through alignment rather than scale, that exist beneath the surface until the earthquake, and then everyone asks where it came from.b5

b5

Martin Buber, the philosopher most associated with Jewish existentialism, described the encounter with the Divine as “an inexpressible confirmation of meaning… The question of the meaning of life is no longer there. But were it there, it would not have to be answered.” The statement is remarkable for what it does not do: it does not answer the question. It dissolves it. A system coherent enough to dissolve the question of meaning — rather than answering it with a slogan or a metric — produces people who do not need external validation to know what they are for. That is undercurrent power. It does not announce itself. It does not need to.

And the stereotypes that have followed Jewish communities for millennia — the accusations of disproportionate influence, the conspiracy theories, the villainization — are, in a structural sense, the shadows cast by exactly this kind of undercurrent power. Not because the conspiracies are true. Because the pattern is real, and people who see a pattern they cannot explain with the tools they have will reach for the explanation that requires the least complexity. Conspiracy is the low-resolution version of structural observation. But let’s attempt for higher resolution.b6

b6

The terrain being crossed must be named explicitly because the terrain is where bad faith actors live. The pattern described here — that internal coherence allows a minority population to exercise disproportionate cultural and institutional influence — is real and documented by serious scholars. It is also the exact claim antisemites make when constructing conspiracy theories. The difference is not in the observation but in the explanation. The antisemite says: they are conspiring to control. The structural observation says: their system is coherent, and coherent systems produce influence disproportionate to their numbers. Conspiracy requires coordination. Coherence requires only alignment. The conflation of the two is itself a symptom of the low-resolution thinking that conspiracy provides.

Now consider the opposite architecture.

Islam and Mormonism — and the violence of placing these two in the same sentence is acknowledged, and the footnotes will do the work the main text cannot — are what might be called coagulating models.b7 This is not a theological judgment. It is an observation about what happens when a system’s internal doctrine contains enough imprecision that regional leaders can interpret it differently.

b7

The brush being used here borders on irresponsible, and that should be named. Islam spans nearly two billion people across dozens of countries with radically different political systems, cultural practices, and doctrinal traditions. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has its own internal diversity invisible to outsiders. The structural observation — that imprecision in doctrine enables regional variation in authority — is defensible as an architectural claim. It is not an adequate description of the lived experience of any individual person within these traditions.

The gray space — the distance between what the text says and what any given leader claims it means — becomes the operating room for power. Different leaders in different regions close the gap differently, and the result is geographic clustering. Regions organized around a particular leader’s particular reading of a doctrine elastic enough to accommodate contradictory readings.

These systems spread. They spread metabolically — absorbing populations, creating identity, generating loyalty. But they clump. They produce many leaders but brittle, regional power, because the same imprecision that lets a leader in one region justify his authority lets a leader in the next region justify the opposite. The imprecision is not a flaw. It is the feature that enables rapid growth. But it is also the feature that prevents deep coherence, and without deep coherence, power fragments.

The imperfections themselves give the leaders grace in their villainy. They can close the gap between what they do and what their doctrine demands because the doctrine itself has enough gray to permit the closure. A precise system constrains its leaders. An imprecise system enables them. The trade-off is durability. The precise system produces fewer leaders but power that lasts millennia. The imprecise system produces many leaders but power that is regional, generational, breakable.b8

b8

This insight has been explored in political science through selectorate theory — Bueno de Mesquita and colleagues’ The Logic of Political Survival (2003) — and in organizational behavior through principal-agent theory. The observation holds across secular institutions as well: any organization with a vague mission statement will produce leaders who interpret the mission to serve their own interests. Precision constrains leaders. Imprecision enables them. This is not unique to religion. Religion is simply the oldest and most visible example of the pattern.

And then there is Christianity, which is the most important case study of the three, because it is the system that was supposed to be holding the civilization where this young man grew up.

Christianity, in any era when this essay might be read, is a system perpetually susceptible to coherence failure.b9

b9

The incoherence is measurable. Research organizations have documented for over a decade the growing gap between self-identified Christians’ stated beliefs and their political behavior — particularly on issues of immigration, poverty, and the use of force. The phrase “Christian nationalism” is itself an acknowledgment of the gap: the modifier nationalism is doing work that the noun Christian cannot do on its own, because the text — the actual text, the Gospels — does not support what the political movement bearing its name demands. When a system needs a modifier to explain itself, the system has lost coherence.

Its text struggles to justify its institutional behavior. The same book that says love your neighbor as yourself and whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me is, in every generation, deployed to support policies that its own internal logic cannot sustain. One can watch Christian voters struggle to reconcile their scripture with their politics — on questions of borders, of war, of allegiance to foreign states. The confusion is visible. The incoherence is performed publicly by people who sense the gap but cannot close it either intellectually or emotionally.

Agents of the state deporting strangers. The Sermon on the Mount saying I was a stranger and you invited me in. Bombs falling on distant cities. The Gospels saying blessed are the peacemakers. The Christian mind trying to reconcile these things and finding that the gap is unbridgeable — not because the politics are wrong or the scripture is wrong, but because the system that is supposed to connect them has lost its internal alignment. The doctrine points one direction. The behavior points another. And in between, there is gray — the same gray that enables leaders in the imprecise systems, now eating the system from the inside.

The fracture has names in every generation. In one era, the conservative commentator rooted in a coherent minority tradition calls opponents of a war “cowards, liars, and America haters.” He fires a colleague after months of disagreements over foreign allegiance, and the colleague leaves saying she is “finally free.”b10 The populist broadcaster with a different architecture — America first, foreign entanglements are traps — aligns with strategists who accuse foreign leaders of manipulating domestic policy. The figure caught between them — once a crown jewel of institutional media — sits in the middle, unwilling to condemn outright, unwilling to endorse fully.b11

This framing — one party fracturing, the other never having formed — is reductive and should be named as such. The reform-minded party has had moments of genuine coherence: transformative coalitions, civil rights eras, charismatic campaigns. But each of those coherences was temporary and built around a specific leader or crisis rather than a durable doctrinal architecture. When the leader left or the crisis passed, the coherence dissolved. The opposing coalition, for all its current fractures, was held together for decades by a doctrinal framework — free markets, strong defense, traditional values — that functioned as internal architecture. The reform party’s equivalent has never been successfully built. The “big tent” is a description of incoherence marketed as a virtue.

The party of reform knows what it cares about. It cares about justice, equity, inclusion, the vulnerable, the marginalized. These are not small things. Many of them are the right things. But caring is not coherence. Coherence is when the doctrine and the behavior align — when what the system says and what it does and what it rewards all point the same direction. And the reform party’s idealism has become the thing that prevents its pragmatism. The party that says it stands for the working class defends institutions — academia, credentialing systems, bureaucracy — that working-class people experience as obstacles. The party that says it believes in open debate treats internal dissent as heresy. The party that says it cares about affordability cannot stop talking about things its own voters rank below the price of groceries.b15 The idealism is real. The architecture to deliver on it is not. One party is a building cracking. The other is a blueprint that was never built. Both failures feed the same vacuum. And the vacuum does not care which party fills it. The vacuum cares only that the adolescent looking for an answer to what am I for gets one — and the most coherent answer currently available may well be coming from a website that measures clavicle width.

And a fourteen-year-old boy sitting in a Catholic school during a pandemic — a school whose mission was the development of the whole man — absorbed that incoherence the way children absorb everything. Not through argument. Through architecture. He did not reject the school’s teaching because he disagreed with it. He rejected it because it did not cohere. The words and the experience did not match. And when he went looking for something that did match — something where the doctrine and the behavior and the reward system all aligned — he found a website.

The website was wrong about almost everything. But it was coherent.

And coherence, not correctness, is what adolescents need.b16

The distinction between repair and innovate as optimization targets is not semantic. It produces fundamentally different civilizational architectures. A system optimized for innovation measures progress by what is new. A system optimized for repair measures progress by what is whole. The technology sector’s ethos — “move fast and break things” — is the diametric opposite of tikkun olam, and the civilization each produces looks exactly the way one would expect.

Part Three: The Gear

Power is not macro control.c1

c1

The academic version of this insight is in James C. Scott’s Seeing Like a State (1998): institutions designed to serve populations systematically reorganize those populations to serve the institution. But Scott is describing the macro. The micro — the gear inside the machine — is the individual who cooperates with the reorganization because they believe the institution is protecting something they cannot protect alone. The institution does not need to be right. It needs to be convincing. And convincing, at the individual level, means coherent.

That sentence has a way of circling — the kind of insight that arrives at inconvenient hours and keeps returning because it is the thing the entire argument is trying to prove. The systems just described — Judaism’s precision, the gray space in coagulating models, Christianity’s fracture — those are macro. Those are civilizational. But none of them run on civilizational fuel. They all run on the same single gear, and the gear is not a population or a doctrine or a book.

The gear is a human being deciding what they will protect and what they will tolerate to protect it.

Every system of power that has ever existed runs on this mechanism: a person who believes, consciously or not, that the structure they inhabit will protect the thing they love most. And they will tolerate — even defend — enormous costs if the structure has convinced them it is the wall between them and loss.

A father in a bombed city — one whose daughter’s school was struck on the first day of a war someone in a room named with two words designed to sound decisive.c2 He would do anything to protect that child. A father in the American interior who sleeps with a rifle behind his bedroom door would do the same. A mother in a coastal megacity. A grandfather in a rebuilt European capital. The instinct is not cultural. It is biological — kin selection, confirmed across every society that has ever been studied.c3 Every reasonable human being on earth, given the choice between the death of a million strangers and their child, will choose to spare their child. Every single one.

The literature is extensive and need not be repeated here, but the point bears restating: the instinct to protect one’s own at the expense of the distant is not a cultural artifact. It is the biological gear that every cultural system is built around. The systems differ. The gear does not.

What differs is not the instinct. What differs is the system that tells each person what counts as protecting the thing they love.

For the father in the interior, the system says: arm the boundary. Draw the line between mine and not-mine and make it visible. For the father in the bombed city, the system said: trust the regime, trust the leaders who claim to speak for God, trust that the structure will keep your daughter safe. For the boy at fourteen, the system — the system that actually reached him, the one with the coherence — said: make yourself so attractive that the world cannot ignore you, because attention is the only currency that will protect you from irrelevance, and irrelevance is the thing that will take away your joy.

That is what the boy was protecting. Not his collarbones. His joy. The irony is not found in the performance, but in the obvious failure this coherent ideology has had on the stability of this individual.c4

c4

Jonathan Haidt’s work on moral foundations is relevant here. The shift from traditional community to impersonal, contractual society does not produce freedom — it produces a void that manifests as either lethargy or rage. Young men without coherent systems of meaning experience what Haidt describes as the consequence of removing the structures that once gave purpose without replacing them with anything equally functional. The boy’s optimization for clavicle width is not lethargy and it is not quite rage. It is something worse. It is precision in the absence of purpose — the meticulous, disciplined, self-destructive pursuit of a metric that the void made available and the culture did nothing to compete with.

Every system described in the previous part runs on this gear. The coherent minority community’s internal alignment produces a population that organizes around shared obligation — tikkun olam, repair — because the system has convinced its members that alignment is what protects their families, their communities, their continuity across millennia. The chaotic religious system produces leaders who exploit gray space because the system has convinced its members that loyalty to this particular leader’s interpretation is what stands between them and destruction. The failing system produces confusion because it can no longer convincingly explain what it is protecting or how.

And the appearance-maximizing system — the one that captured a boy in his bedroom during a pandemic — runs on the same gear. He optimizes for clavicle width because the system he entered told him, with perfect internal consistency, that physical attractiveness is the wall between him and a life of being unseen. And being unseen, in the architecture of a boy who is online fourteen hours a day, is the closest thing to death.

This is where the essay earns its right to ask the question it has been circling.

Sit at a dinner table. Any dinner table. Across from anyone — a partner, a parent, a friend, a stranger one respects enough to ask an honest question. And ask them:

What are you optimizing for?

Not what do you believe. Not what are your values. Not what do you want. Those are room questions — the kind that produce answers rooms can metabolize. The question being asked here is mechanical. What is the optimization target? What is the thing being arranged around, whether consciously or not?

Most people will say something about happiness. Or family. Or peace. And they will mean it. And they will not be wrong.

But the follow-up is the one nobody can answer, and it is the one that matters:

Do you know why you chose that?c5

c5

This question — do you know why you chose that — is the one that separates structural observation from therapy. The point is not to produce guilt about the answer. The point is to ask whether the answer is genuinely one’s own or whether it was installed by a system never examined. Most people’s answers were installed. That is not a criticism. That is how systems work. The question is whether the system that installed the answer was coherent enough to install a good one.

Because rooted within every answer to what are you optimizing for is a system that taught the answer. And the system did not teach through argument. It taught through coherence — through the alignment, or misalignment, between what the adults and institutions in a life said and what they did. The coherent systems produced people who know what they are optimizing for and can articulate why. The incoherent systems produced people who are optimizing for something they cannot name, taught by a system they cannot see, defending a joy they have never examined.

The young man can name his optimization target. He can name it with extraordinary precision. Clavicle width. Facial harmony ratios. Sexual market value. The specificity of his answer is the most damning thing about it — because it means the system that taught him was coherent enough to be exact, in a world where every other system in his life was too incoherent to even try.c6

c6

This could be read as an argument for authoritarianism — for rigid systems that tell people what to think. It is the opposite. The distinction between a coherent system and an authoritarian system is whether the system can survive the question. Authoritarian systems provide answers and forbid the question. Coherent systems — the best of them — invite the question and provide answers robust enough to survive it. Judaism’s tradition of Talmudic argument, of wrestling with God’s own instructions, is itself a form of coherence: the system is strong enough to contain its own challenges. The appearance-maximizing movement, by contrast, is coherent but brittle — it provides answers but cannot survive scrutiny, which is why it operates in spaces that reward spectacle over examination. The question is not whether systems should provide answers. Every system provides answers. The question is whether the answers can survive the dinner table.

Part Four: The Transfer

The most patient ones win.d1

d1

This observation is about undercurrents — the patience of systems that do not need to announce themselves because their coherence lets them accumulate power below the surface. The Jewish diaspora is the clearest historical example. But the corporate version is happening now, and the systems building the undercurrent model are not the ones making noise.

That is the thing about undercurrents. One does not see them coming. One feels them after they have already reshaped the coastline. The systems described above — the coherent ones, the ones that produce durable power — do not announce themselves. They do not need to. They operate below the surface of whatever civilization they inhabit, accumulating influence through alignment rather than scale, until one day the ground shifts and everyone asks where it came from.

The ground is shifting now. A new coherence is forming. It is not religious. It is not national. It is corporate.

This is not a metaphor.d2

d2

Carolyn Chen, a sociologist at UC Berkeley, documented this in her research on technology industry work culture. Her finding: workplaces have become “the new faith communities” where workers find “identity, meaning and belonging — the social and spiritual needs that Americans once turned to their religions to fulfill.” This creates what she calls a “theocracy of work.” Her research shows this extends across major corporate America. The structural elements of religious organizations — mission, code of ethics, origin story, charismatic leader — are now standard corporate architecture. Source

Something happened over the last generation that we still do not have adequate language for. Corporations became the rooms where people find identity, meaning, belonging, and purpose — the things that were once sought in churches, communities, families. Most major corporations now have a mission, a code of ethics, an origin story, and a charismatic leader. They use words like passion, authenticity, mission, purpose, joy to describe work — words that were once reserved for non-economic institutions, for families and faith communities that people gave unconditional love and loyalty to.d3

d3

The language is the tell. When a corporation describes work using words once reserved for families and faith communities — passion, mission, purpose, joy, authenticity — it is performing a transfer of coherence. It is saying: the meaning once found in a church, a family, a community, can be found here. And for many people, it delivers. The corporate coherence produces identity. It produces belonging. The question is whether a corporation — which is architecturally required to optimize for shareholder value — can sustain the kind of coherence that survives contact with the dinner table question.

The mechanism is identical to the one described in the previous part. Coherence creates power. A corporation that aligns its doctrine — the mission statement — with its behavior — what it actually rewards — produces loyalty that functions identically to religious loyalty. The employee who says I believe in what we are building is running the same gear as the congregant who says I believe in what God is doing. The optimization target changed. The gear did not.

But here is where it becomes dangerous. Because two models of corporate coherence are forming simultaneously, and they map precisely onto the two models of religious coherence already described.

Some of these new systems are building the precision model — the undercurrent model. Internally aligned. Self-aware of what they are. Operating below the surface of public attention, accumulating capability, pursuing coherence not through expansion but through depth.d4 The technology builders that are quiet. The infrastructure architects who do not give public talks. The patient ones. They exist as undercurrents until the earthquake.

d4

Specific companies are deliberately unnamed because naming them would collapse the observation into a debate about those companies, and the observation is prior to any individual company. The structural point: some technology systems are building internal coherence that mirrors the precision model — aligned purpose, patient accumulation, self-awareness about what they are. Others are building the chaotic model — vague mission, exploitable gray space, founder-wieldable. The question of which model produces the technology that shapes the next century is not a technology question. It is a coherence question. The same question that determined whether a religion became an undercurrent or a coagulation. The tools changed. The mechanism did not.

Others are building the chaotic model — the coagulating model. Enough gray space in the mission that any leader can justify any behavior. Enough imprecision in the values that founders can close the gap between what the company says it is and what it actually does. Move fast and break things. The doctrine is vague enough to absorb any contradiction. The leader can always find room.

The question the essay has been building toward: which model is winning? And does anyone inside these systems know which one they are building?

Because the transfer is already complete for the generation that produced the young man. And this is the observation that may be the most important in this essay — the one that needs the footnotes to hold it because it sounds absurd in the main text until one sees the engineering:

The appearance-optimization movement is not a hobby. It is not a trend. It is not an internet fad. It is a religion.d5

d5

The structural comparison is defensible point by point: doctrine, ritual, priesthood, heresy, afterlife promise, tithe, community. Each element maps. The comparison reveals that this movement fills the exact functional role in its adherents’ lives that religion fills in others’ — identity, community, purpose, hierarchy, hope. Whether one is comfortable calling it a religion is less important than recognizing that it is doing the same job, for the same reasons, using the same gear.

It has doctrine: the blackpill philosophy, which holds that physical attractiveness is the primary — perhaps the sole — determinant of social and romantic success. It has rituals: mewing, bonesmashing, steroid cycles, caloric restriction, daily measurement of progress against objective metrics. It has a priesthood: the most prominent optimizers at the top, the rated faces of the forums in the hierarchy below, an entire caste system of who has ascended and who has not. It has heretics: anyone who claims looks do not determine value is accused of “cope” — the movement’s term for self-deception, its version of apostasy. It has an afterlife promise: ascension — the state of having optimized one’s appearance to the point where social power flows without effort.

It has tithes. The most prominent figure charges fifty dollars a month. He earns six figures from subscriptions and donations. The faithful pay to learn the path to ascension.

The system is internally coherent. Perfectly coherent. Its doctrine aligns with its behavior. Its promises match its demands. Its metrics are measurable. Its rewards are immediate. It answers the question what are you optimizing for with a precision that no religious school, no megachurch, no secular institution, no political party, no corporation is currently matching for boys between the ages of twelve and twenty.

The fact that its answer is clavicle width tells everything about what happens when the old coherences fail and the vacuum fills with whatever moves fastest.d6

d6

The University of Portsmouth researchers documented how this content evades platform moderation by appearing to be about self-improvement rather than ideology. Source This is structurally identical to what was described in “The Architecture” — the root system finding new soil after the room closes. The original ideology could not survive moderation. The rebranded version can. The room changed its name. The architecture continued.

And the algorithm — the infrastructure through which all of this circulates — does not care which coherence wins. It cares that coherence produces engagement. The algorithm metabolizes Judaism and appearance-optimization and Christianity and corporate mission statements and war footage and bonesmashing tutorials into the same currency: attention. Attention is the new tithe. The new tithe feeds the new rooms. And the new rooms optimize for their own continuation the way every room has always optimized for its own continuation — not maliciously, architecturally.d7

d7

This is the development that keeps circling: the moment where the room no longer needs reality to manufacture its cause. In recent conflicts, AI-generated footage of attacks that never happened circulated alongside footage of attacks that did, and the distinction mattered less every day. In this movement, the AI analysis apps that the leading figures promote — apps that rate facial harmony, that calculate “sexual market value” — are manufacturing the metric by which boys measure their worth. The tool creates the standard. The standard creates the need. The need creates the market. The algorithm promotes what people watch. People watch what makes them feel something. The room runs itself.

The pattern described in “The Architecture” — media rooms, military-industrial rooms, political rooms, all metabolizing events into content, contracts, campaigns — here the pattern is the same but the transfer is further along. The appearance-optimization room metabolizes adolescent insecurity into revenue. The corporate identity room metabolizes human longing for purpose into labor. The technology room metabolizes human knowledge into capability.

But capability is not coherence. And the difference is the whole game.d8

d8

Capability without coherence is what Krypton had. The essay will come back to this.

Part Five: The Table

If a civilization wants to rise — and the word is used deliberately, because the opposite is what happens when coherence fails — it needs to ask a different question than the one the algorithm is currently answering for its children.

Europe rose through the Enlightenment not because Europeans were smarter but because they built institutions coherent enough to channel human curiosity into something cumulative. Asia rose through Confucian, Buddhist, and communal traditions coherent enough to sustain discipline across generations without burning out the people inside them. Judaism survived — not merely survived, persisted as an undercurrent through every civilization that tried to erase it — because it gave its people an answer to what are you optimizing for that was precise enough to be actionable, flexible enough to survive exile, and honest enough to include its own uncertainty.

The answer was not be great. The answer was not be powerful. The answer was not be attractive.

The answer was: the world is unfinished. You are here to help finish it. Start with what is closest to you.

That is an answer a fourteen-year-old can hold. It is specific. It is immediate. It tells a person what to do tomorrow morning. And it does not require belief in God — it requires only the belief that something is broken and that one is capable of fixing a piece of it.

Christianity, at its best, once offered something structurally similar. Love your neighbor. Feed the hungry. Visit the prisoner. Actionable. Immediate. Coherent. But the system has lost its internal alignment, and a system that cannot match its doctrine with its behavior cannot answer an adolescent’s question, no matter how beautiful the doctrine is. The beauty is irrelevant if the architecture does not hold.

Other traditions have offered structure — intense, all-encompassing, community-saturated structure. And for many people they work. But the imprecision in their doctrine creates the gray space already described, and the gray space is where people fall through. The system is too leader-dependent, too regional, too shaped by whoever happens to be wielding it in a given place and time. It produces belonging without producing the kind of coherence that survives the dinner table question.

And the secular West — the culture that was supposed to replace religion with reason — produced the appearance-maximizing movement. It produced a generation of boys who measure their jaw angles with calipers because nobody offered them anything more coherent to measure.

Wars are the macro version of this failure. A civilization can spend hundreds of billions of dollars in weeks and cannot answer the question what are we optimizing for in a way that survives contact with dead children in a school called The Good Tree. The argument is always about the tax on the tea. It has always been about the tax on the tea. And while the argument continues, the vacuum fills.

What goes into the vacuum is whatever is most coherent. At present, that is the algorithm. And the algorithm’s answer to what are you optimizing for is: whatever keeps you watching.

The algorithm does not care if one is watching a bonesmashing tutorial or a sermon or a war. It cares that one watches. And the boys — the fourteen-year-olds, the ones who are right now sitting in their bedrooms during the next pandemic or the next crisis or the next fracture — will go where the coherence is. They will always go where the coherence is. That is what the gear does. That is what the gear has always done. That is what the gear will do for the next child, and the one after that, and the one after that, until someone builds something better.

So the question is not whether those boys will find an answer. They will. The question is whether the answer they find will be clavicle width or repair.

That is the dinner table. That is where it has to start. Not in a policy paper. Not in a sermon. Not in a school mission statement. At the table, across from someone loved, asking the simplest question anyone can ask:

What are you optimizing for?

And this time, having an answer that coheres.

Epilogue: The Farm

Superman was conceived in 1933 and published in 1938 by two Jewish teenagers in Cleveland.e1

e1

The concept of Superman was first developed in 1933 by Jerry Siegel (writer) and Joe Shuster (artist) while both attended Glenville High School in Cleveland, Ohio. The character was not published until Action Comics #1, cover-dated June 1938 — five years of rejection before the concept reached print. The Encyclopedia of Cleveland History and the Ohio History Connection both date the character’s creation to 1933. Siegel and Shuster were sons of Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe. Source Source

Jerry Siegel and Joe Shuster. Sons of immigrants from Eastern Europe. Bespectacled, aware of their otherness. Siegel said the idea came to him on a sleepless night — a character like Samson, like Hercules, like Moses, all in one. Shuster drew him. They could not sell the concept for five years.

The character they made is the most famous fictional person in human history, and the thing most people miss about him is the thing that matters most.

Superman’s power comes from Krypton. His morality comes from Kansas.

Kal-El is sent from a dying planet by parents who cannot save themselves. He lands in a field. He is found by Jonathan and Martha Kent — not philosophers, not theologians, not intellectuals. Farmers. People who grow things. People whose answer to what are you optimizing for is so simple it barely registers as philosophy at all: take care of what is in front of you. Do the right thing. Your abilities are gifts, not weapons.

That is it. That is the entire moral framework that contains a god.

DC Comics has explored, more than once, what happens when the rocket lands somewhere else. In the Crime Syndicate stories — Earth-3, the mirror universe — the baby is found by people who see his power and see a weapon. They keep him isolated. They keep him afraid. They exploit what he can do. He becomes Ultraman. The most powerful being on his planet and its greatest villain. Same Kryptonian body. Same yellow-sun strength. Different farm.

Superman is not Superman because of Krypton. Superman is Superman because of the Kents. Because two farmers in Kansas looked at something with incomprehensible power and decided the right thing to do was not to weaponize it, not to monetize it, not to optimize it — but to raise it. To sit at the dinner table with it every night. To show it, through the coherence of their own small ordinary lives, what a person is for.

And here is the detail that closes the loop of this entire essay — the one that is easy to almost miss, the one that makes the question being asked feel as though it has an answer even if the answer cannot yet be fully seen.

Superman’s birth name is Kal-El. In Hebrew — the language of the people who created him — El means God. The name has been interpreted as “Voice of God,” “All that God is,” “Vessel of God.”e2 The character that two Jewish kids built in a bedroom in Cleveland during the Depression — modeled on Moses, sent from destruction to safety, raised with his identity concealed among strangers until the moment the world needed him — is, at the level of its very name, a Jewish story about what power is for.

e2

Kal (or Kol, depending on accent) means “voice” or “light” in Hebrew. El means “God.” The name Kal-El has been translated as “Voice of God” among other interpretations. While there is no definitive documentation that Siegel and Shuster consciously intended this Hebrew reading, both creators were Jewish, and the character’s parallels to the Moses narrative — sent from destruction, hidden identity among strangers, raised to save a people — are extensively documented in scholarly analysis. The Hebrew resonance, whether intentional or absorbed from the tradition the creators inhabited, is precisely the kind of undercurrent this essay describes. Source

It is tikkun olam in a cape.

The most famous answer to what should power optimize for in the history of fiction was written by two sons of Jewish immigrants who understood, from the coherence of their own tradition, that the answer is not dominance. It is not expansion. It is not attention. It is not the width of one’s collarbones. It is repair. The world is unfinished. You are here to help finish it.

And the entire premise — the load-bearing structural premise of the most enduring myth the twentieth century produced — is that the answer does not come from Krypton. It comes from the farm. From the table. From two ordinary people who looked at unlimited power and chose to raise it instead of aim it.

This essay has been about coherence as the mechanism of power. About religions as engineering diagrams. About corporations as new systematic identities. About a young man who traded his fertility for the width of his collarbones because nobody in his life offered him a coherent alternative. And the essay’s own machinery — the footnotes, the structural observations, the civilizational comparisons — risks committing the exact sin it is trying to diagnose. It risks making the answer too complex.

The answer is not complex.

Philosophy — if it has any ability to impact the actual world that actual people actually live in — earns that ability only when it is pragmatic enough to realize something that most philosophers are too vain to admit: most people do not think deeply about these questions. Not because they are stupid. Not because they lack the capacity. Because they prefer a simpler life. And they are right to. The simple life — the life where one grows things and raises things and sits at the table and does the right thing — that is not the consolation prize for people who cannot handle complexity. It is the point. It is the thing that complexity is supposed to serve.

The Kents are not intellectuals. They are farmers. The moral code they gave Clark is almost embarrassingly simple. Do the right thing. Take care of what is in front of you. Your strength is a gift, not a weapon. There is no footnote. There is no recursive structure. There is no civilization-spanning theory of coherence. There is a kitchen table in Kansas and two people who meant what they said and lived accordingly.

That simplicity is what survives contact with godlike power. Not the theology. Not the philosophy. Not the theory. The table.

Every intellectual tradition that has lasted — Judaism, Confucianism, the best of the Enlightenment, the best of every wisdom tradition that has ever survived longer than a generation — understood this. The complexity exists to produce simplicity. The scholarship exists to build a world where most people do not need to be scholars. The argument exists to build a table where most people can simply sit.

The duty of the person who does think deeply — the person reading this essay, the person writing it — is not to drag everyone else into the complexity. The duty is to build the farm. To build the institution, the family, the coherence that is strong enough and simple enough that when a kid with enormous potential lands in it, the kid becomes Superman and not Ultraman. Not because the kid is extraordinary. Because the farm was coherent.

Not out of pity for the people who prefer a simpler life. Out of duty to the world their simplicity holds together.

The boy landed on a forum because that was the farm that was there. It was coherent. It answered the question. It told him what he was for. And it made him into a weapon — a weapon aimed at himself, at his own body, at the boys who follow him, at the very idea of what a young man could become if someone had simply sat across from him and said: the world is unfinished, and you are built to help finish it.

There is a young man who made himself infertile for the width of his collarbones. He was raised in a Catholic school that promised to develop the whole man. He was given a culture that could not answer the only question he was asking.

And he went where the coherence was. Because that is what the gear does. That is what the gear has always done. That is what the gear will do for the next adolescent, and the one after that, and the one after that, until someone builds a better farm.

Superman was conceived by two Jewish kids in 1933 and published in 1938. The whole story — the entire myth — is that power without coherence is catastrophe, and coherence comes from the farm, and the purpose of power is to repair what is broken. That is tikkun olam. That is the oldest answer to the oldest question. And two teenagers from Cleveland understood it well enough to build the most famous character in human history around it, and to make the whole thing simple enough for Kansas.

The world is unfinished.

Build the table. Sit at it. Mean what you say.

And when the next kid lands — and the next kid is always landing, right now, tonight, in a bedroom with a screen and a question and no one sitting across from him — make sure the farm is coherent enough that the answer he finds is not clavicle width.

Make sure the answer is repair.
